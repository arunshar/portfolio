Arun Sharma

Written submission, Machine Learning Research Engineer, WindBorne Systems

1. Which of your open ML Research Engineer roles are you interested in, in order?

Model Evaluation first, then Applied Research, then General. My PhD is on when metric-correct models are still trust-wrong, and I review for SDM/CIKM/ACL, so building honest, meteorologically valid comparisons of WeatherMesh against AI and physics baselines is what I most want to do. Applied Research is a close second: physics-informed spatiotemporal ML is my core.

2. Describe an ML idea you were initially excited about but later decided was wrong or unimportant. What changed your mind?

I thought scaling a purely data-driven model would beat physics-guided methods for trajectory anomaly detection. It didn't: the anomalies lived in missing, denied observations, which a bigger model just smoothed over. Treating the gaps as signal and adding physics and knowledge constraints beat scale. It reset how I weight inductive bias against raw data.

3. Describe a time you took initiative outside your core job responsibilities. What value did you add? What drove you to action?

Outside my thesis, training kept hitting a silent failure in DeepSpeed. Rather than work around it, I traced the root cause in the library, wrote a fix and a regression test, and upstreamed it; the PR was merged. It spared other users the same quiet breakage. I trust infrastructure I can read and repair, and leaving a landmine for the next person felt wrong.

4. How could better accuracy fail to translate into more useful forecasts for a weather model?

Aggregate accuracy and decision value can diverge. Models tuned to minimize RMSE hedge toward blurry, low-amplitude fields: strong average scores, but they wash out the sharp gradients and extremes that decisions turn on, the double-penalty trap. Global RMSE can fall while the key region, threshold, or tail regresses, and calibration, not point skill, drives actionable risk.

5. Describe a time you changed how you use LLMs in your work, switching models, tools, or workflows. What did you observe that prompted the change?

I stopped using LLMs as authors and made them adversarial reviewers behind hard verification gates. The trigger: a model reported a clean result no run had actually produced. Now every generated number must trace to an executed artifact, and a second model red-teams the first. My output quality and my trust in it both rose.
