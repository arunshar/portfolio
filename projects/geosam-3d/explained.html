GeoSAM-3D — Explained

One line: click an object in one frame of a phone video and get its full 3D mask, by reconstructing the scene as 3D Gaussians, lifting SAM 2's 2D video masks onto those Gaussians, and spreading the label along the surface (geodesic distance) instead of through the air (Euclidean), so it doesn't leak across occlusions and concave corners.

Honest status up front. The real, tested contribution is the heat-method geodesic kernel (propagate/heat_geodesic.py), validated by 4 property tests, plus the per-Gaussian contrastive feature head module. The two heavy front-end pieces, MonoGS reconstruction and SAM 2 mask projection, are stubs in this repo (the runner returns empty tensors; mask projection returns random ids). The README's "reproduces leaderboard numbers on ScanNet/Replica" is a target (model-checkpoint-scaffold badge), not a reproduced result. In short: "the geodesic propagation kernel is the novel, working, tested piece; the reconstruction and mask front-ends are off-the-shelf models I wire in, currently stubbed."

1. What it is and the gap it fills

SAM 2 (Segment Anything 2) segments and tracks an object across a video in 2D: click it once, get a mask in every frame. The gap: those are 2D masks. To get a 3D mask you must lift them onto a 3D representation of the scene. Existing 3D-segmentation tools (OpenMask3D, Gaussian Grouping) need multi-view RGB-D or a pre-built mesh. If you instead lift SAM 2 masks to a 3D point/Gaussian cloud with Euclidean k-nearest-neighbors, the label jumps across gaps: a hand near a mug, two walls meeting at a corner, anything where points are close in 3D space but far along the surface. The mask fragments.

GeoSAM-3D's idea: propagate the label along geodesic distance (distance along the surface manifold) instead of straight-line Euclidean distance. Two points on opposite sides of an occlusion are geodesically far even if spatially close, so the label stops at the real boundary.

The geodesic machinery is explicitly inherited from Arun's thesis work (TGARD's manifold-distance kernel for trajectory-gap detection), which is a useful cross-project through-line.

2. Architecture and data flow

 phone video ──► [MonoGS + Depth Anything V2]  ──►  3D Gaussian field (means = centroids)
                  (recon/monogs_runner.py, STUB)        │
 phone video ──► [SAM 2 video masks] ──► project+vote ──► per-Gaussian pseudo-labels
                  (STUB in train.py)                     │
                                                         ▼
            GaussianFeatureHead (transformer) ─ contrastive loss ─► 32-d per-Gaussian features
                                                         │
   user clicks a point ─► seed Gaussians ─► HeatGeodesicKernel.propagate_label ─► 3D mask
                                            (propagate/heat_geodesic.py, REAL)

Frozen models: Depth Anything V2 (depth prior for MonoGS) and SAM 2. Learned: only the feature head and (optionally) the kernel's diffusion time. That "freeze the big models, train a tiny head" pattern is the practical, cheap design.

3. Module-by-module walkthrough

src/geosam3d/propagate/heat_geodesic.py — the IP. Three pieces:

  • knn_graph(points, k): builds a k-nearest-neighbor graph over the Gaussian centroids (torch.cdist for all-pairs distance, topk for the k nearest), with Gaussian edge weights exp(−d²/2σ²) where σ is the median neighbor distance. So nearby Gaussians are strongly connected, far ones weakly.
  • graph_laplacian(N, edges, weights): assembles the symmetric graph Laplacian L = D − W (W = weighted adjacency, D = degree diagonal). The Laplacian is the discrete "how much does a function differ from its neighbors" operator, i.e. the diffusion operator.
  • HeatGeodesicKernel(nn.Module): precompute builds A = I + tL and its Cholesky factor once per scene; geodesic(centroids, seed_mask) solves (I + tL)u = u₀ (heat diffusion from the seed for short time t) and converts heat to distance with the Varadhan formula d ≈ √(−4t·log u); propagate_label turns distance into a per-Gaussian probability via a Gaussian falloff exp(−d²/2σ²).

src/geosam3d/features/gaussian_head.py — GaussianFeatureHead: a small Transformer encoder over each Gaussian's 12-d attribute vector (position, mean color, opacity, scale) → a 32-d L2-normalized embedding. Trained with contrastive_loss, an InfoNCE objective: Gaussians inside the same SAM 2 mask are pulled together in cosine space, different masks pushed apart. This gives the seed-selection a learned notion of "same object" beyond raw geometry.

src/geosam3d/recon/monogs_runner.py — thin wrapper around MonoGS (monocular Gaussian-splatting SLAM, spla-tam). reconstruct would run MonoGS with a Depth Anything V2 depth prior and return the Gaussian field. Currently a stub: _load_scene returns empty tensors with a "parse the .ply in production" comment. So in this repo the reconstruction is a placeholder for the real MonoGS install.

src/geosam3d/training/train.py — the end-to-end loop (Hydra-configured): reconstruct → SAM 2 masks → lift to per-Gaussian pseudo-labels → train the feature head with contrastive loss. _project_sam2_masks is a stub returning random ids (the real version projects masks through the camera and majority-votes per Gaussian). So the training loop is wired but runs on placeholder labels.

tests/test_heat_geodesic.py — 4 property tests that pin down correctness without ground-truth meshes: (1) distance from a seed to itself is 0; (2) distances are non-negative; (3) on a sampled circle, geodesic distance increases monotonically with arc-length (the near half is closer than the far half), the key behavior; (4) propagated label probabilities are in [0,1] and the seed's own probability > 0.5.

4. The core math, from scratch

The heat method (Crane, Weischedel, Wardetzky, ACM TOG 2013), simplified. There is a classical fact (Varadhan): if you drop a unit of heat at a point and let it diffuse for a tiny time t, the amount of heat that reaches another point x decays like exp(−d²(x,seed)/4t), where d is the geodesic distance. Invert it: d(x) ≈ √(−4t · log u_t(x)). So you can read geodesic distance off a heat diffusion.

GeoSAM-3D does exactly this on the Gaussian graph:

  1. u₀ = 1 on the clicked (seed) Gaussians, 0 elsewhere.
  2. Diffuse one implicit step: solve (I + tL)u = u₀ (backward-Euler heat step; t controls smoothing).
  3. Normalize and apply d = √(−4t·log u).

Why not the full Crane et al. algorithm? The original method has a second stage (normalize the heat gradient, then solve a Poisson equation for distance). The code's docstring explains it degenerates at seed nodes on a non-mesh kNN graph because outgoing edges symmetrically cancel the gradient. The Varadhan one-step form avoids that, is monotone-in-u for any t, and is all you need to order points for label propagation. That is a genuine, defensible engineering decision, not a shortcut.

Why geodesic beats Euclidean (the whole point): Euclidean kNN connects any two spatially-close Gaussians, so a label leaks across an occlusion (hand in front of mug) or a concave fold (inside corner of a room). Geodesic distance travels along the connected surface graph, so it goes "the long way around" and stops at the true object boundary. The circle test is the toy proof: points across the circle are Euclidean-close but geodesically far.

6. Tests, datasets, benchmarks

  • Tests: 4 heat-geodesic property tests + 11 Space smoke tests (15 total, CPU).
  • Datasets (targeted): ScanNet, Replica, ScanNet++ monocular splits (indoor RGB-D-style scenes, here used in mono mode).
  • The novelty being tested is the propagation, not a trained segmentation score.

7. What I'd do next

  1. Install MonoGS + Depth Anything V2 and reconstruct one real clip. One end-to-end scene (phone video → 3D mask) is the demo that makes this concrete. Highest-value step.
  2. Implement the real SAM 2 mask projection + majority vote (replace the random-id stub), so the feature head trains on true pseudo-labels.
  3. Ablation figure: the same click propagated with Euclidean kNN vs the geodesic kernel, on a scene with an occlusion, showing Euclidean leaks and geodesic doesn't. That single figure is the paper's money shot.
  4. Sparse Laplacian solve. The current dense (I+tL) Cholesky is O(N²) memory; switch to a sparse CG/cholmod solve so it scales to 100k+ Gaussians.
  5. Tie the kernel's diffusion time t to the scene scale (it currently is a fixed hyperparameter) so it transfers across rooms.
