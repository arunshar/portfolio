PhysFlow-Earth — Explained

One line: super-resolve satellite imagery and climate fields with a generative model (rectified flow + a Diffusion Transformer), but add a physics penalty so the sharp output it invents still obeys the conservation laws of the data, precipitation mass, divergence-free wind, Sentinel-2 band ratios, instead of hallucinating physically-impossible textures.

Honest status up front. This is the CV-vocabulary repackaging of Arun's own published work (Pi-DPM, the Kriging-informed conditional diffusion paper at SIGSPATIAL 2024, GCDM at SIGSPATIAL 2025, and the PC-RF/AIAS paper). The physics residual operators, the rectified-flow training step, and the DiT backbone are real and tested (8/8 physics + flow tests pass, the DiT forward runs). The trained checkpoints and the benchmark numbers (PSNR 28.6 beating EDiffSR) are scaffold/targets (two "checkpoint scaffold" badges). Because the method is Arun's published diffusion line, this is the project he can defend most deeply, the math is the same idea as the PC-RF paper, just written in "rectified flow + DiT" instead of "conditional diffusion."

1. What it is and the gap it fills

Diffusion/flow super-resolution makes images look sharp by inventing plausible high-frequency texture. For natural photos that's fine. For scientific fields it's dangerous: an invented precipitation texture can violate mass conservation (the fine field's average no longer equals the coarse input), an upsampled wind field can have non-physical divergence, and a sharpened Sentinel-2 image can break the band ratios (NDVI, NDWI) that downstream vegetation/water indices depend on. A "beautiful" but physically-wrong field is worse than a blurry-but-correct one.

PhysFlow-Earth's idea: keep the generative power (rectified flow + DiT) but add a differentiable physics-residual loss that punishes those violations during training, evaluated on the model's predicted clean sample at every step. The model learns to be sharp and conservation-respecting, without a separate projection or Lagrangian solver. It's the exact thesis of the PC-RF/AIAS paper, generalized to three modalities.

2. Architecture and data flow

 coarse field x_lr  ──► LR tokenizer ─┐
                                      ▼
 noisy x_t, time t  ──► patch embed ─► DiT-XL blocks  ──► velocity v_theta(x_t,t,c)
                                      ▲   (cross-attend to a 64-entry
 physics codebook ───────────────────┘    physics-constraint codebook)
                                      │
                    hat_x_1 = x_t + (1-t)·v_theta   (one-step clean estimate)
                                      │
                    PhysicsResidual(hat_x_1, x_lr) ─► band-ratio / divergence / mass residuals
                                      │
   loss = ||v_theta - (x_1 - x_0)||²  +  λ·||R(hat_x_1)||²

The physics term is evaluated on hat_x_1 (the clean image the model would produce now), and its gradient flows back through the velocity head, so the model is pushed toward conservation-respecting predictions directly.

3. Module-by-module walkthrough

src/physflow/flow/rectified.py — RectifiedFlow. The training step: sample noise x_0, a random time t, form the linear interpolant x_t = (1−t)x_0 + t·x_1, predict velocity v_pred, and compute the velocity loss ‖v_pred − (x_1−x_0)‖² (rectified flow matches the straight-line velocity from noise to data). Then the one-step clean estimate hat_x_1 = x_t + (1−t)v_pred, the physics residual on it, and the hybrid total. Returns a FlowStepOutput with both losses.

src/physflow/physics/operators.py — the differentiable building blocks: average_pool (project HR→LR grid for mass/band residuals), central_diff_x/y (shape-preserving central-difference derivatives via replicate-padding), horizontal_divergence(u,v) = ∂u/∂x + ∂v/∂y, upsample_nearest. All pure PyTorch so gradients chain through them.

src/physflow/physics/residual.py — three modality-specific residual heads sharing one interface:

  • BandRatioResidual (Sentinel-2): the average-pooled NDVI/NDWI of the super-resolved image must match the LR NDVI/NDWI. NDVI = (NIR−RED)/(NIR+RED), NDWI = (GREEN−SWIR)/(GREEN+SWIR). So sharpening can't break the vegetation/water indices.
  • DivergenceResidual (ERA5 wind): penalizes ∂u/∂x + ∂v/∂y (synoptic wind is near-incompressible; non-zero divergence = invented sources/sinks of air).
  • MassConservationResidual (CHIRPS precip): avg_pool(SR) − LR must be ~0, so the fine field preserves the coarse total (no invented/destroyed rain).
  • PhysicsResidual composes them into a weighted L2 sum, with per_residual for logging.

src/physflow/models/dit.py — DiTVelocity, a Diffusion Transformer (Peebles & Xie 2023) with two extensions: (1) the LR field is tokenized and concatenated to the patch sequence (conditioning), and (2) each block cross-attends to a learned 64-entry "physics codebook." DiTBlock uses adaLN-Zero modulation (time-conditioned shift/scale/gate), self-attention, the codebook cross-attention, and an MLP; the head un-patchifies via pixel_shuffle back to image shape. Sinusoidal time embedding feeds the adaLN.

tests/test_physics_residuals.py — 6 correctness tests that pin the operators: avg-pool inverts nearest-upsample (so mass residual is exactly 0 on a perfect upsample); divergence is 0 on a constant field and recovers a known linear gradient; mass and band-ratio residuals are 0 when HR is a perfect upsample of LR. These prove the physics operators are exactly what they claim, so the loss means what it says.

Other dirs: flow/sampling.py (Euler/few-step ODE inference), flow/adjoint.py (adjoint-mode gradient variant), models/{conditioning,pipeline}.py (LR tokenizer, physics codebook, from_pretrained Diffusers-style pipeline), data/{worldstrat,sen2venus,era5_chirps}.py, eval/{worldstrat_bench,physics_bench}.py.

4. The core math, from scratch

Rectified flow in one breath. Instead of diffusion's many noisy steps, learn a velocity field v_θ(x_t,t) that moves a sample along the straight line from noise x_0 to data x_1: x_t = (1−t)x_0 + t·x_1, and the target velocity is the constant x_1 − x_0. Train by regressing v_θ onto that. At inference you integrate the ODE dx/dt = v_θ in a handful of Euler steps (far fewer than diffusion's hundreds). It's the same generative idea as the PC-RF paper.

The physics trick. At any time t, the model's implied clean prediction is hat_x_1 = x_t + (1−t)v_θ (extrapolate the straight line to t=1). Evaluate the physics residual R on that, and add λ‖R(hat_x_1)‖² to the loss. Because hat_x_1 is differentiable in v_θ, the model is trained to make predictions whose physics residuals are small, no test-time projection needed (though you can add one). This is exactly the PC-RF "soft penalty on the one-step clean-field estimate" mechanism, here for three EO modalities.

Why these three residuals? Each is a real conservation law of its data: precipitation can't be created/destroyed at the domain scale (mass), large-scale wind is near-incompressible (divergence-free), and Sentinel-2 indices must survive sharpening (band ratios). They're the same family as the AIAS paper's divergence/non-negativity/mass penalties.

6. What I'd do next

  1. Train one modality end to end (Sentinel-2 ×4 on WorldStrat) and report PSNR + the physics-violation metric with vs without the residual term, the latter is the whole selling point and your own metric.
  2. Show the failure it prevents: a side-by-side where the no-physics model breaks NDVI and the physics model doesn't. That's the paper figure.
  3. Lean on the PC-RF connection: this is your published method; cite Pi-DPM / Kriging-diffusion / PC-RF and frame PhysFlow as the rectified-flow + DiT generalization, that's a strength, not a gap.
  4. Cut DiT-XL to DiT-B for a runnable demo (XL is large); a small model that demonstrates the residual mechanism is more convincing than an untrained XL.
